II. OPTIMIZATIONS
The time complexity of DEBY RAD renders it computa-
tionally prohibitive for large molecules, requiring optimiza-
tions for practical use. Many existing methods are designed
for full scattering analysis, accounting for deviations from
the idealized algorithm such as hydration layers, beam non-
linearity, and excluded volume effects. The excluded volume
correction, for instance, accounts for solvent displacement
by the molecule of interesta necessary consideration since
SAXS is often performed in-situ [9]. However, these are
outside the scope of this review and will only briey be
mentioned for each optimization.
A. Parallel and Distributed Processing
The most straightforward optimization is parallel and dis-
tributed processing. FoXS (Fast X-Ray Scattering) is a widely
used web server that rapidly computes SAXS proles from
atomic coordinates using the Debye formula, tting computed
proles to experimental data by adjusting excluded volume and
hydration layer density parameters [10]. Nearly all algorithmic
approaches use parallel processing to some degree given
the ease of implementation and the large improvements in
execution time.

III. A NEW APPROACH
The techniques outlined in the previous section either re-
quire complex mathematical formulations and/or substantial
computational resources for parallel and distributed process-
ing. Additionally, they remain tied to physical representation
of the algorithm as a summation over atoms and coordinates.
To the best of the authors knowledge, no existing approaches
treat the scattering prole I(Q) as a purely computational
object, abstracted from molecular geometry and atomistic
models. The aim of this section is not to directly compete
with these highly optimized implementations, but instead to
provide an abstraction of the algorithm for future development
of novel approximation strategies.
